The Wild Deep-Space Radio Signal Astronomers Still Can't Fully Explain

On the night of 15 August 1977, a radio telescope at Ohio State University briefly picked up one of the strangest signals in the history of astronomy: a sharp, 72‑second spike at precisely the hydrogen line frequency, now famous as the “Wow! signal.” The Big Ear radio telescope had been scanning the sky for signs of extraterrestrial intelligence, but this one transmission stood out so dramatically that astronomer Jerry Ehman circled the numbers on the printout and scribbled “Wow!” in the margin. It was far stronger than the background noise, appeared only in one of the telescope’s channels, and rose and fell in a way that suggested it was coming from a fixed point in space, not from passing aircraft or local interference.

The Indian Island That's Being Eaten by the Sea in Front of Everyone's Eyes

In the lower Hooghly estuary of West Bengal, not far from Kolkata, a small island called Ghoramara has become one of India’s starkest examples of slow, relentless climate damage. Once covering more than 7 square kilometres, Ghoramara has shrunk dramatically over the last few decades as rising seas, powerful cyclones, and shifting river channels steadily gnaw away at its soft, muddy shores. Villages shown on older maps now lie underwater, and entire hamlets have been abandoned as the coastline retreats metre by metre.

The 'Goldilocks' Planet 40 Light-Years Away That Might Be Our Best Shot at Another Earth

In 2017, astronomers announced the discovery of seven Earth‑sized planets orbiting a small, cool star named TRAPPIST‑1, roughly 40 light‑years from Earth in the constellation Aquarius. Three of those worlds lie in the star’s so‑called habitable zone, where temperatures could, in theory, allow liquid water to exist on the surface if the planets have suitable atmospheres. Among them, one world — TRAPPIST‑1e — quickly emerged as the most promising candidate for an Earth‑like environment.

The Tiny "Water Bears" That Survived the Vacuum of Space

Tardigrades — often nicknamed “water bears” for their stubby legs and lumbering gait — are some of the toughest animals known to science. Measuring less than a millimetre long, these microscopic creatures can withstand crushing pressures, extreme temperatures, and intense radiation that would kill most other life forms. Their legend grew in 2007, when thousands of tardigrades were launched into low Earth orbit on the European Space Agency’s FOTON‑M3 mission and exposed directly to the vacuum of space.

The Salamander That Regrows Whole Limbs — and What It's Teaching Doctors

Among vertebrates, few animals are as astonishingly regenerative as the axolotl, a Mexican salamander that spends its life in a larval, aquatic form. While humans can only heal small injuries and cannot regrow lost arms or legs, adult axolotls can regenerate entire limbs — including bones, muscles, nerves, and skin — multiple times with little or no scarring. They can also repair parts of their spinal cord, heart, and other organs, making them a central model organism for scientists trying to understand how complex tissues might be coaxed to rebuild themselves.
