The Viral "Dream Man" Everyone Claimed to See — and How It Turned Out to Be a Hoax

SR
Sarah Roberts
June 19, 2025
Culture8 min read

In the late 2000s, an eerie sketch of a man with thick eyebrows, a receding hairline, and a neutral expression began circulating online with a provocative question: “Ever dream this man?” According to the accompanying story, thousands of people from cities around the world had independently reported seeing the same unfamiliar face in their dreams since 2006. The figure, dubbed “This Man” or “the Dream Man,” was said to offer advice, appear at moments of crisis, and haunt the sleep of strangers who had never met him in waking life.

The idea that an identical face could appear in so many people’s dreams fascinated and unsettled readers. The official website, ThisMan.org, claimed that the phenomenon was first noticed by a New York psychiatrist after multiple patients recognized the same sketched portrait, and that the image had since been reported in dreams by thousands of people worldwide. The site presented various speculative theories, ranging from Jungian archetypes and collective unconscious symbolism to supernatural explanations and secret mind‑influencing experiments.

As the image spread through forums, blogs, and media coverage, more people began to feel that they, too, might have seen the face in a dream — a reaction psychologists say is not surprising. Human memory for dream characters is fuzzy, and when people view a generic composite face after reading a striking story, they may retroactively fit their vague recollections to match it. The drawing itself was intentionally designed to look both distinctive and universal, with features that could be interpreted as older or younger, more familiar or more anonymous depending on which parts one focused on.

Eventually, journalists and researchers dug into the origins of the project and discovered a much more mundane explanation. Italian sociologist and marketer Andrea Natella later admitted that “This Man” was a piece of conceptual art and guerrilla marketing, not a documented psychological mystery. The website had been created in 2008 as a deliberate hoax, crafted to see how far a spooky, open‑ended narrative could spread and how people would react when presented with the suggestion that they might share a hidden experience.

Even after the hoax was exposed, the face of This Man continued to circulate in horror videos, creepypasta stories, and social media posts, taking on a second life as a kind of modern urban legend. For some, the fact that it was invented only made it more interesting, because it highlighted how easily suggestion and viral storytelling can shape people’s memory and sense of personal experience. The saga has since been used in discussions of how internet myths form, how marketing campaigns can blur into folklore, and how the brain constructs meaning from ambiguous images.

So while there is no credible evidence that a single, mysterious man is visiting people worldwide in their dreams, the “This Man” episode does reveal something genuine: when a story taps into existing anxieties about the mind, identity, and shared experience, many will find echoes of it in their own lives — even if it began as a carefully staged illusion.
