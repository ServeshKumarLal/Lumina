The Hidden Drawings Lurking Beneath Famous Paintings

CV
Catherine Voss
June 26, 2025
Culture10 min read

To museum visitors, Old Master paintings usually appear as finished, immaculate surfaces, their visible brushstrokes carefully preserved behind glass. Yet infrared imaging has revealed that many of these works conceal earlier sketches, abandoned figures, and subtle changes that artists never expected anyone to see. Using a technique called infrared reflectography, conservators can peer through some paint layers to detect carbon‑based underdrawings and alterations, turning static masterpieces into records of evolving ideas.

Infrared reflectography works because different materials absorb or reflect infrared light to varying degrees. Light‑coloured grounds bounce infrared wavelengths back, while carbon‑rich underdrawing materials like charcoal or ink absorb them, appearing dark in the resulting images. When technicians scan a painting panel or canvas line by line, they can produce a mosaic image that shows the hidden drawing beneath the final paint, along with any changes in composition that were painted over during the work’s creation.

Examinations of European paintings from roughly 1500 to 1800 have shown that revisions, or *pentimenti*, are common rather than rare. In some cases, artists shifted the positions of hands, altered clothing, or changed facial features; in others, they replaced entire figures or rethought the background architecture. Infrared studies of Jan van Eyck’s “Arnolfini Portrait,” for example, have revealed adjustments to contours and details that offer insight into how the painter refined his composition as he worked. Similar imaging of portraits from the Tudor period has uncovered underdrawings that differ significantly from the final painted designs, suggesting that artists sometimes granted themselves more freedom than the rigid, iconic surfaces might imply.

These hidden layers can have practical implications, too. Differences between underdrawing and surface paint can help scholars distinguish between an original work by a master and a later copy that simply replicates the visible image. Discovering a confident, exploratory underdrawing that shows corrections and experimentation often supports the idea that a painting is a primary work, while a mechanically precise match between underdrawing and surface may indicate that an assistant followed a template without improvisation.

For the public, exhibitions that juxtapose visible images with their infrared counterparts offer a rare glimpse into the creative process. Visitors can see early marks that were never meant to show, compare ghostly outlines with the final composition, and appreciate how much thinking and revising went into works that might otherwise feel static and inevitable. Rather than diminishing the magic of the paintings, these scientific tools tend to deepen it, revealing the human hesitations, corrections, and decisions that lie just beneath the varnish.

Infrared technology is now standard in major museums’ conservation departments and continues to uncover surprises even in well‑studied collections. Each new underdrawing or hidden figure reminds viewers that masterpieces are not just images but layered objects — objects that hold inside them earlier versions of themselves, visible only when viewed with the right kind of light.
