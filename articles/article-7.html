The Real Buried City Beneath Seattle's Streets

JR
James Richardson
June 12, 2025
History9 min read

In downtown Seattle’s Pioneer Square, tourists can buy tickets for “Bill Speidel’s Underground Tour,” a guided walk through a network of buried sidewalks and storefronts that once formed the city’s street level. The existence of this underground grid is not an urban legend; it is the result of a chaotic rebuilding effort after the Great Seattle Fire of 1889 destroyed around 30 blocks of the young city. When Seattle decided to raise its streets to combat chronic flooding and sewage problems, the first floors of many buildings were literally left below the new pavement.

After the fire, city leaders opted to rebuild in brick and stone and to elevate the street grade by as much as 8 to 35 feet in some places. Construction progressed unevenly, so for several years pedestrians navigated a strange two‑level city: they could walk on the old sidewalks at the original ground level, or climb ladders up to the new, higher streets above. Eventually, the spaces between the old sidewalks and the new road surface were roofed over, turning former ground‑floor storefronts into basements and creating a continuous underground passageway along several blocks.

For a time, parts of this buried city remained in use for storage, small businesses, and unofficial gathering places, but by the early twentieth century most entrances had been sealed due to safety concerns and changing building codes. The underground spaces fell into disrepair, accumulating debris, discarded fixtures, and evidence of the city’s early plumbing and electrical systems. In the 1960s, writer and preservationist Bill Speidel campaigned to save Pioneer Square’s historic buildings and opened the first tours into the sealed passages, helping spur the area’s designation as a historic district.

Today, the Underground Tour takes visitors beneath modern sidewalks to see crumbling brick walls, old storefront facades, and glass “skylights” set into the pavement that once allowed daylight to reach the buried walkways. Guides point out remnants of old bank vaults, saloon entrances, and cast‑iron columns that supported the original city, alongside stories of colourful characters, vice districts, and the rough‑and‑ready infrastructure of a frontier port. What was once an embarrassing tangle of forgotten basements has become one of Seattle’s strangest tourist attractions.

The story of Seattle’s underground city highlights how quickly urban landscapes can change — and how much of their history can end up literally buried in the process. For locals, it can be jarring to realize that everyday streets sit atop an older layer of roads, doorways, and shop windows that were simply entombed rather than demolished. For visitors, the tour offers a rare chance to walk through a preserved cross‑section of nineteenth‑century city life, complete with the uneven floors, low ceilings, and patched‑together engineering that modern building regulations would never permit.

Unlike some tales of “cities beneath cities,” Seattle’s buried streets are fully documented and accessible with a ticket, not a rumour. Yet the experience of stepping into them — one level down from the present, among the ghosts of streets that no longer exist on maps — still feels surprisingly surreal. It is a reminder that major metropolises often have physical memories layered beneath their surfaces, waiting to be rediscovered.
