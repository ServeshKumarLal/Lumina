Inside the Real Luxury Bunkers Billionaires Are Building for the Worst-Case Scenario

TW
Thomas Wright
June 5, 2025
Culture13 min read

Over the past decade, reports have surfaced of ultra‑wealthy tech founders and investors quietly buying remote properties and commissioning high‑end survival bunkers, turning doomsday preparation into a status symbol. Silicon Valley insiders have described this trend as “apocalypse insurance,” suggesting that a significant share of the rich now keep some form of plan — from private islands to underground shelters — in case of catastrophic social or environmental breakdown. New Zealand, with its isolation and political stability, has become one of the favoured destinations for such projects.

Companies that once built hardened facilities for missiles or government command centres now market “survival condos” carved into old missile silos and underground complexes designed for long‑term, comfortable living. One well‑publicised project in Kansas converted a decommissioned Atlas missile silo into multi‑storey luxury apartments complete with shared pool, rock‑climbing wall, cinema, and even a dog park; penthouse units have been advertised in the multi‑million‑dollar range. Other firms, such as Rising S Bunkers and Vivos, offer bespoke shelters equipped with gyms, greenhouses, medical rooms, and advanced air filtration, with some large models priced in the millions.

Investigations by outlets such as *Wired*, the *BBC*, and independent journalists have described building plans and permit documents that list sprawling “basements” or “storage” areas which, according to local workers, are in practice fortified underground compounds. One widely discussed example is a massive project in Hawaii tied to Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg, reportedly including blast‑resistant doors, its own food and energy systems, and strict non‑disclosure agreements for contractors. Tech figures such as Peter Thiel have also been linked to ambitious retreat or bunker plans in New Zealand’s South Island mountains.

Critics argue that this bunker boom reveals a troubling mindset among some of the world’s most powerful people: instead of investing primarily in strengthening public institutions and shared resilience, they are devoting significant resources to personal escape options. Writers like Douglas Rushkoff, who has briefed wealthy clients on future risks, describe meetings where executives show more interest in protecting themselves from angry populations after a collapse than in preventing collapse in the first place. The phenomenon has become a symbol of deep inequality — a world where a few can picture riding out disaster in hidden comfort while everyone else faces it exposed.

Defenders of such preparations reply that contingency planning is rational, and that private bunkers are simply an extreme form of the same instinct that leads others to store food, buy generators, or keep emergency supplies. They also note that some hardened facilities could, in theory, shelter essential staff and equipment needed to help societies recover after a major catastrophe. Still, the secrecy around many of these projects and the scale of luxury involved make it difficult to separate legitimate risk management from a broader retreat from social responsibility.

Whatever one’s view, the existence of these shelters is no longer purely speculative. Real missile silos have been converted into guarded underground communities, real bunkers have been shipped to remote ranches and islands, and real fortunes are being sunk into preparations for scenarios that most people prefer not to think about. The question they raise is not only what the rich fear might happen, but what those fears — and the decisions they drive — might mean for the rest of society if crises actually arrive.
